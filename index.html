Case study

Improving inventory accuracy with a scan-first, location-based workflow

A product redesign for warehouse teams who work through interruptions, movement, and physical constraints instead of tidy screen-by-screen flows.

The original system created avoidable mistakes because it asked staff to remember too much, translate digital tasks into physical space, and work linearly in an environment that never is.

Lead Product Designer Mobile web app 2023

The problem

High defect rates were a workflow problem, not a training problem.

Inventory operations were seeing high order defect rates and low inventory accuracy because the system did not support how work actually happened on the floor.

01

Loose workflows

Users had too much freedom in critical tasks, which made it easy to skip steps or improvise around guardrails.

02

No physical mapping

Staff had to translate between the warehouse and the interface manually because digital tasks were not anchored to real locations.

03

Interrupted work

Flows assumed uninterrupted, linear work even though tasks were fragmented and staff regularly switched context midstream.

Even simple tasks became error-prone because the system expected tidy execution in a messy environment.

Understanding the real workflow

The team researched movement, interruptions, and momentum instead of just screens.

Interviews, on-site observation, and working alongside facility staff revealed that inventory work is location-based, fragmented, and dependent on continuity.

Methods

Mobile pop-up usability labs in facilities and hands-on observation as food runners.

Behavior

Staff navigated inventory by location, scanning, moving, and updating items as they went.

Design implication

The product needed to support non-linear work and preserve context between interruptions.

Product definition

The redesign centered on three moments and one shared operating system.

Partnering with operations introduced a color-coded, QR-based location model so the digital product could finally understand where work was happening.

Moment 1

Receiving stock

Inventory data was being lost before items were correctly placed and validated.

Moment 2

Order scan-out

Counts became inaccurate because fulfillment updates happened after the physical action.

Moment 3

Stocktake

Counting was too disruptive and depended on people remembering what to do next.

Scan-first workflows that follow physical movement Capture data at the moment of action System-driven tasks instead of memory-based work

Design solution

Guided inventory receiving turned fragmented work into a recoverable, location-based flow.

Problem

Receiving was not location-based and lacked critical data and status visibility.

Support interrupted work

Granular statuses let staff mark items as arrived, save progress, and resume later.

Guide by location

QR-coded racks turned receiving into a linear sequence tied to the physical environment.

Capture data at source

Expiration details were entered when stock arrived, not retroactively after memory faded.

Errors are prevented during receiving instead of being discovered afterward.

Scan-first fulfillment

Picking became a guided physical path instead of a memory test.

Problem

Order errors happened when staff relied on memory and inventory was updated after picking.

Enforce the right sequence

Users were guided through the correct shelf, rack, and item order before confirming quantities.

Handle edge cases in context

Missing or substituted items required a reason so stock updates stayed honest in imperfect scenarios.

Sync action with data

Inventory counts were updated at the exact moment the exception happened, not after the run.

Inventory stays accurate as orders are fulfilled.

Stocktake milestone 1

The first win was making stocktake executable in the physical space.

Problem

Stocktake was time-consuming and mismatched with staff behavior on the floor.

Align counting with space

Staff counted by location using the color-coded system instead of navigating long SKU lists.

Embed adjacent actions

Move-inventory and waste flows were built directly into counting so extra workflows disappeared.

Resolve discrepancies immediately

Errors were handled in the moment while people were already at the shelf.

Stock accuracy improved because discrepancies were resolved immediately instead of later.

Stocktake milestone 2

The bigger shift was turning stocktake into a system-driven daily workflow.

Problem

Even after the flow improved, stocktake still depended on staff remembering when and where to do it.

Push tasks instead of asking for planning

The system generated location-based work automatically, removing setup overhead from the team.

Follow the daily list

Users simply worked through assigned tasks instead of manually assembling a stocktake session.

Keep continuity across interruptions

Progress was saved so the team could resume without losing state or creating inconsistency.

Stock accuracy no longer depends on dedicated stocktake events.

Outcome

The tool became part of daily operations because it matched how the floor actually works.

Facility staff adopted the redesigned workflows in daily use. Scan-first fulfillment reduced order defects by preventing errors during action, and inventory tasks became location-driven, consistent across shifts, and less dependent on individual memory.

Adopted

Integrated into daily operational routines rather than treated as a special project.

Preventive

Errors were stopped during picking and receiving instead of corrected after the fact.

Consistent

Tasks became repeatable across shifts because the system carried the workflow memory.
